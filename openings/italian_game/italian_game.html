The Italian Game

The Italian Game is a very popular opening in Chess, especially for new players as it follows all the basic principles of openings - controlling the centre, developing pieces and protecting the king.

  1. e4 e5
  2. Nf3 Nc6
  3. Bc4

The move 3. Bc4 develops the light squared bishop into an excellent tile for it to apply pressure on the weak f7 pawn, potentially also preparing Ng5.

3...Nf6 (Two Knights Defense)

3...Nf6 transposes into a Two Knights Defense, resulting in complex and open play. See: Two Knights Defense

3...Bc5 (Giuoco Piano)

3...Bc5 intends to stop white's central expansion by adding another defender to the d4 square to ensure that white is unable to play d2-d4. In this situation, white can choose to immediately play d4 and gain an edge in development of his minor pieces, or he can prepare it with moves like c3 or b4.

4. b4 (Evans Gambit)

The Evans Gambit is an extremely aggressive line, where white attempts to sacrifice a pawn to deflect black's c5 bishop from defending the d4 square.

If black accepts, c3 followed by d4 breaks the centre open forcibly.

If black declines, white's b4 pawn stakes out space on the queenside for white and possibly allows white to gain a tempo by threatening to trap black's bishop.

4. d3 (Giuoco Pianissimo)

In this opening, white aims for a slow buildup and delays d4 until he can prepare it. This leads to a heavily strategic, positional game where exchanges happen later in the game rather than earlier.
